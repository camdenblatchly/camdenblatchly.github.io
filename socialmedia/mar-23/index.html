Camden's Data Corner

After attending the Charlotte Urbanists walking tour, my curiosity was further piqued by this graphic which compares housing permits per capita across major metropolitan areas. I was curious to see how these metrics compared at the county level, so I recreated the graphic for a subset of counties I was interested in.

As you can see in the chart above, housing permits per capita in Mecklenburg County (home to Charlotte) far outpace the permits per capita in San Francisco County and Suffolk County (home to Boston).

It's also interesting to note that counties with tourism-based economies (La Plata, Carroll, and Grafton) still aren't issuing as many housing permits as they were prior to the great recession.

How to hike in a cableknit sweater

(A young Camden, dressed for success!)

Upon moving to England in the summer of 2007, I quickly began the process of assimilation. I learned the rules of cricket, rugby, and field hockey. I incorporated key pieces of slang (chav, cracking, mate) into my vocabulary, and, most importantly, I ditched my cargo pants and t-shirts for an itchy wool blazer and school tie that I wore to class every day.

Thus began my relationship with ‘preppy’ clothing, a realm of style so rife with connotations and cultural associations that seven episodes are needed to fully explore its origins and legacy in Avery Trufelman’s excellent podcast American Ivy. Over the course of the series, Trufelman traces how a style originating on ivy league campuses was alternately championed by Japanese teenagers, civil rights activists, Jewish entrepreneurs, and Brooklyn rappers.

While ivy style has become the default business casual uniform, it wasn't always associated with formality; rather, ivy style rose to prominence because it was a casual and comfortable alternative to the wool suits that were the male uniform of the time.

This contrast between current and past notions of formality is why I love photos from the late 20th century. Outfits that appear prim to our modern eye were once as unceremonious as a tracksuit.

I especially adore old photos of hut croos. While their clothing choices were (presumably) driven by practicality, when I see old photos of croo hiking in fisherman sweaters and khaki shorts, I can’t help but find their outfits wonderfully decorous. In this era of rampant consumerism, these photos are a reminder that before we had Gore-Tex, Spandex, and other "technical" garments, people still managed to hike and live in the mountains.
